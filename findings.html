Our Findings
1. Introduction

In this section, we'll tell you about our findings based on data from the New York Taxi dataset. A comprehensive analysis of this data provides insights into the workings of the New York City taxi industry and the behaviour of its customers.

Now, let's begin!

2. Data Analysis
2.1 Fare Distribution

Let's start by looking at the distribution of passenger payments to drivers across the various dimensions!

This figure shows the distribution of taxi trip fares in the $0 to $100 range, revealing that the majority of trips are relatively cheap.

From the above figure we can see that the distribution of fares shows a clear right skewed pattern, indicating that most of the journeys have relatively low fares. And this pattern is common in the taxi industry as most passengers tend to use taxis to complete short journeys. Based on the peak data in the histogram, we can surmise that this is the distance from the centre of New York City to the surrounding area, as this is the most common travel distance in the city. Although the maximum value of the fare is set to $100 in this figure, there are in fact many outliers, such as fares in excess of $200. But these outliers are small in number, perhaps reflecting the cost of longer trips or peak times. In addition to this, the KDE curve provides a smoothed view of the fare distribution, further confirming the observation that the majority of journeys have lower fares. The curve peaks at lower fares and then gradually declines, indicating that the number of trips with lower fares is much higher than the number of trips with higher fares.

This boxplot visualises the distribution of taxi journey costs by hour of the day, revealing fluctuations and outliers at different times of the day.

From the above figure we can see that the median fluctuates between time periods, which means that the mainstream travelling distances are different or the traffic conditions are variable at different times of the day. The outliers in the box plot mean that there are journeys that are longer than normal or that traffic conditions are poor at that time of day. In addition to this, the 5am box has the longest length, meaning that costs fluctuate more at this time of day. Shorter boxes mean that the costs are more similar for that time of day.

This figure clearly shows the average cost of a taxi journey for each hour of the day, revealing the fluctuating cost trends during the day and night.

The chart above shows how the average fare changes over the course of a day. We can notice a significant rise in fares at 5am again, which could be due to an increase in costs due to a reduction in supply.

Combining the three figures, we can find some discoveries about the fare:

1. Most New York taxi fares are relatively low amounts because short journeys are more common in the city.

2. Taxi fares are higher in the early morning hours, mainly due to the imbalance between supply and demand caused by fewer taxi drivers.

3. Fare fluctuations are highly correlated with traffic conditions.

The above findings can help taxi operating companies to better plan the operating hours of taxis as well as the number of taxis to be operated, and at the same time help passengers to plan their travelling time wisely. More importantly, it can help city administrators to arrange the public transport in the city in a more rational way, and to reduce the tailpipe emission in order to reduce the heat island effect and air pollution in the city.

2.2 Pick-up Locations Distribution

The video above shows how pick-up locations change at various times of the day. As you can see from the video, during the hours of work, i.e. from 8am to around 4pm, pickups are concentrated in Manhattan, with relatively few pickups in the surrounding areas. At other times of the day, pickups in the surrounding areas increase significantly. This trend is mainly due to the fact that people need to travel between office buildings in Manhattan during working hours. At other times of the day, the demand for travel increases, so it is no longer concentrated in Manhattan.

Therefore, a more reasonable option for taxi operators would be to increase the number of taxis in the Manhattan area during working hours, while during the rest of the day they could reduce the number of vehicles in the Manhattan area and spread them out more to meet different needs. In addition, city administrators could also increase the number of public transport operations in the Manhattan area during working hours to allow commuters to choose greener travel options.
